The document in 60 seconds

Xefe calculates pay, tax and social security for Timor-Leste businesses. This document shows every formula it uses, with worked examples, so a local accountant can confirm the numbers are right before businesses rely on them. The defaults are:

  • Wage Income Tax (WIT): residents pay 10% of monthly wages above $500; non-residents pay 10% of everything.
  • Social security (INSS): employee 4%, employer 6% of the contribution base (overtime and most allowances excluded).
  • Extra pay: overtime 150%, rest days and public holidays 200%, night hours +25% on top of normal pay.
  • Deductions: loans, advances and court orders together may not push total deductions past 30% of wages, and no line is ever deducted beyond the amount actually owed.
  • 13th month (Subsidio Anual): one month's salary, proportional to months worked, payable by 20 December.
  • VAT: switched off — Timor-Leste has not enacted VAT.

What we need from you: read sections 2–9 (each is short and has a worked example), answer the sign-off questions in section 11, and complete the conclusions table and signature block at the end. The 15 questions are where the law allows more than one reading and we need a professional decision — the three most important are the scope of the 30% deduction ceiling, the weekly-pay method, and which allowances are genuinely tax-free reimbursements.

1. Scope and calculation controls

Included in this review Payroll earnings and deductions; resident/non-resident WIT; employee and employer INSS; payroll journals; invoice and bill totals; payments; journal balancing; ledger-derived reports.
Outside this review Corporate income tax, import duty, excise, inventory costing, asset depreciation, foreign exchange, and sector-specific rules. Those require separate specifications if enabled.

Money precision

Implemented Money operations use Decimal.js rather than binary floating-point arithmetic.

Stored/displayed money = intermediate value rounded to 2 decimal places using ROUND_HALF_UP

Line values are rounded to cents and totals are built from the rounded lines. This makes invoices, payslips and journals reproducible. ROUND_HALF_UP is not “banker’s rounding” (ROUND_HALF_EVEN); the reviewer should confirm the preferred local payroll convention.

Accounting integrity controls

  • Every journal line is normalized to cents.
  • A line must contain a debit or a credit, never both and never neither.
  • Negative journal amounts are rejected.
  • Stated totals must equal the sum of lines, and total debits must equal total credits.
  • Payroll posting is idempotent by payroll-run source, preventing a second approval from duplicating the journal.
  • Payments must be at least $0.01 and cannot exceed the remaining balance.

2. Payroll calculation order

Implemented The engine calculates each employee in this order:

  1. Determine regular pay for the selected pay frequency.
  2. Add overtime, night premium, rest-day/holiday pay, sick pay, allowances and annual subsidy.
  3. Sum all earning lines to obtain gross pay.
  4. Calculate absence and late-arrival reductions.
  5. Derive wages paid, WIT taxable income and the INSS contribution base.
  6. Calculate WIT, employee INSS and employer INSS.
  7. Apply the 30% interpretation to court-ordered and voluntary deductions.
  8. Calculate net pay and total employer cost.
  9. Save the rate/base/residency snapshots used by statutory reports.
Gross pay = sum(all earning lines)
Wages paid = max(0, gross pay − absence reduction − late reduction)
Net pay = gross pay − sum(all payslip deductions, including attendance reductions)
Employer cost = wages paid + employer INSS

Regular pay

Hourly employee regular pay = hourly rate × regular hours
Monthly employee regular pay = monthly salary
Weekly/biweekly salary payment = monthly salary ÷ actual pay dates in that month

Confirm method The current weekly/biweekly method makes all pay dates inside one calendar month add back to one monthly salary. A different common method is monthly salary × 12 ÷ 52 (weekly) or monthly salary × 12 ÷ 26 (biweekly), producing a constant payment. The employment contract and accountant should determine which method applies.

Hourly equivalent for a salaried employee

Monthly standard hours = 44 hours/week × 52 weeks ÷ 12 months = 190.666666… hours
Hourly equivalent = monthly salary ÷ 190.666666… → rounded to cents

Example: $1,000 ÷ 190.666666… = $5.244755… → $5.24/hour. Premium calculations currently use the rounded $5.24 rate. Confirm rounding point

3. Wage Income Tax (WIT)

Implemented Default rates follow current ATTL guidance.

Resident employee — monthly period

WIT taxable amount = max(0, taxable wages − $500.00)
Resident WIT = WIT taxable amount × 10%

Example: taxable wages $800.00 → ($800.00 − $500.00) × 10% = $30.00 WIT.

Non-resident employee

Non-resident WIT = taxable wages × 10%

Example: taxable wages $800.00 × 10% = $80.00 WIT.

Periods shorter than one month

Period threshold = $500.00 ÷ actual pay dates in the pay month
Period WIT = max(0, period taxable wages − period threshold) × 10%

Example for four weekly payments: threshold $500 ÷ 4 = $125.00; taxable wages $200.00; WIT = ($200.00 − $125.00) × 10% = $7.50.

WIT reporting data

Paid payroll records retain taxable income, the amount actually subjected to WIT, WIT withheld, residency status, and year-to-date values. Monthly and annual reports prefer these saved snapshots rather than trying to reconstruct old payroll using today’s rates.

Filing timing: ATTL states that withheld wage income tax is paid by the 15th day after the month of withholding. The employer annual WIT form is due by 31 March following the tax year.

4. Social Security (INSS)

Implemented The mandatory default rates are:

Employee INSS = contribution base × 4%
Employer INSS = contribution base × 6%
Total INSS remittance = employee INSS + employer INSS

Example: contribution base $550.00 → employee $22.00; employer $33.00; total remittance $55.00.

Contribution base

The base is built from earning lines marked contributable, less related unpaid absence/late time. An explicitly saved contribution base can override the derived base for a record. A payee marked INSS-exempt has a zero base.

Derived INSS base = max(0, contributable earnings − absence reduction − late reduction)
Included under current INSS guidanceExcluded under current INSS guidance
Base salary; annual/13th-month subsidy; performance-related salary increases; night, shift and remote-location supplements. Overtime; bonuses, commissions, gratuities and profit sharing; food subsidy; extraordinary allowances; representation; qualifying transport, board, lodging and travel subsistence.
Employer timing: the remuneration declaration is submitted from the 1st to the 10th of the following month; contribution payment is made from the 10th to the 20th.

The night calculation is an additional 25% because those hours are assumed to have already been included in regular salary/hours. Rest-day and holiday entries are currently paid as a separate 200% line. Confirm input convention

Sick leave

Daily equivalent = rounded hourly equivalent × 8 hours
  • Annual sick days 1–6: 100% of daily equivalent.
  • Annual sick days 7–12: 50% of daily equivalent.
  • Beyond day 12: no employer sick-pay line under the current rule.

Paid payroll history supplies year-to-date sick days so the tier does not reset each run. For a salaried employee, the engine adds the eligible sick-pay line and removes the absent hours; the two movements net to full or half pay as applicable.

Annual leave and maternity/paternity defaults

  • Annual leave: at least 12 working days.
  • Maternity leave: 12 weeks (84 days), with 10 weeks/70 days necessarily after birth.
  • Paternity leave: 5 paid working days.

Absence and late arrival

Absence reduction = rounded hourly equivalent × absence hours
Late reduction = rounded hourly equivalent × (minutes rounded up to the next 15 minutes ÷ 60)

Employer policy The 15-minute upward rounding is not presented here as a statutory requirement. It should be confirmed in employment rules and checked for fairness/legal compliance.

30% monthly deduction ceiling

Ceiling = wages paid × 30%
Available for court/loan/advance/other = max(0, ceiling − WIT − employee INSS)

WIT and employee INSS are withheld in full. Court orders and voluntary deductions share the remaining ceiling and are reduced proportionally when requested deductions exceed it. Absence/late reductions are treated as remuneration not earned, rather than an amount withheld for a third party.

Interpretation requiring sign-off: Article 42(3) says deductions cannot exceed 30% of remuneration received per month. The current implementation protects WIT and INSS, then applies the remaining ceiling to other deductions. A strict reading could instead count every deduction inside the 30%.

6. Annual subsidy / 13th month

Implemented The subsidy is optional per payroll run and calculated as:

Annual subsidy = monthly salary × min(months worked in year, 12) ÷ 12

Full-year example: $1,000 × 12 ÷ 12 = $1,000.00.
Six-month example: $1,000 × 6 ÷ 12 = $500.00.

It is currently WIT-taxable and included in the INSS base. Labour Law Article 44 states that the annual subsidy is at least one month’s salary, proportional to months worked, and paid by 20 December.

Confirm partial month A hire month currently counts as a whole month; daily pro-rating is not applied.

8. Invoices, bills and payments

Implemented Invoices and bills use one calculation path for preview, save and posting.

Line before discount = quantity × unit price
Line discount = line before discount × discount percentage
Line net = line before discount − line discount
Document subtotal = sum(rounded line net amounts)
Line tax = line net × line tax rate; document tax = sum(rounded line tax)
Document total = subtotal + document tax

Mixed tax rates are supported at line level and tax is rounded per line. The invoice-level rate is only the default for a line without an explicit rate.

Payment state

New amount paid = previous amount paid + accepted payment
Remaining balance = document total − new amount paid

Balance zero → paid; positive balance → partially paid. Zero, negative, non-finite and over-balance payments are rejected.

VAT inactive Timor-Leste does not currently have an active national VAT regime in the product. The VAT return screen is blocked from calculating, saving or filing until a platform-level activation flag is set. Invoice tax-rate fields must not be interpreted as permission to charge VAT. Government planning documents indicate VAT introduction is planned, but future legislation, rates and effective dates must be configured only after official enactment and professional review.

11. Questions requiring accountant / legal sign-off

These are the remaining items I would resolve before describing the system as locally certified.

  • 30% ceiling: Does it include WIT and employee INSS, or may statutory deductions sit outside it as currently implemented?
  • Deduction carry-forward: Should a loan/court-order amount trimmed this month automatically roll into a later payroll?
  • Weekly/biweekly salary: Divide the monthly salary by pay dates in that calendar month, or use constant annualized 52/26-period payments?
  • Pay-period cent reconciliation: Should the last weekly/biweekly payment absorb any one-cent remainder?
  • Hourly precision: Round the hourly equivalent to cents before premiums, or retain extra decimals and round only the final earning line?
  • Night hours: Confirm they are already contained in regular hours, making the extra line 25%, not 125%.
  • Holiday/rest-day input: Confirm whether the separate line should be 200% for both hourly and salaried staff, given how regular hours are entered.
  • Late-arrival policy: Approve or replace the current upward rounding to 15-minute increments.
  • Reimbursements: Confirm when documented transport/travel/food is a reimbursement rather than WIT-taxable wage income.
  • Other earnings: Require explicit WIT/INSS classification before using this catch-all line.
  • Annual subsidy: Confirm whether a partial hire month counts as a full month or must be daily-pro-rated.
  • Minimum wage: Reconfirm that the $115 monthly default has not been superseded for the relevant worker/sector.
  • Custom rates: Decide who may change tenant WIT, INSS and overtime settings, and require an effective date/audit note for any override.
  • Legacy payroll: Spot-check any filing containing records created before statutory base snapshots were stored.
  • VAT: Keep inactive until enacted rules, rate, registration threshold, transitional rules and effective date are independently confirmed.
